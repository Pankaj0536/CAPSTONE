How Storage Devices Work

HDD (Hard Disk Drive) relies on physical spinning disks and moving magnetic heads to read data. SSD (Solid State Drive) uses integrated circuit assemblies (flash memory) to store data electronically.

HDD Storage

Mechanical storage with moving parts

SSD Storage

Electronic storage with zero moving parts

Learning Downside

Why learning theory can feel hard first

The concepts behind disk scheduling are powerful, but they also come with a few drawbacks when you’re still learning.

  • It can feel abstract before you see a real performance simulation.
  • Too much theory at once may make it harder to remember how algorithms behave.
  • Performance depends on actual request patterns and hardware, not just the ideal model.

Hard Disk Drive (HDD)

Mechanical
HDD uses mechanical parts to read/write data
  • The disk (platter) must physically rotate.
  • The arm must physically move to the correct track (Seek Time).
  • This mechanical process creates noticeable delay.

Solid State Drive (SSD)

Electronic
SSD accesses data instantly with no moving parts
  • Data is accessed via electrical grids instantly.
  • No moving parts, completely silent.
  • Vastly superior random read/write speeds.
